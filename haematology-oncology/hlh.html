Hemophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis (HLH)

University Hospitals Bristol and Weston NHS Foundation Trust

Overview

⚠ Critical: HLH is a life-threatening hyperinflammatory syndrome with mortality rates approaching 50% in critically ill adults. Early recognition and prompt treatment are essential.

Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH) is a rare, life-threatening syndrome characterised by excessive immune activation leading to uncontrolled inflammation and multi-organ dysfunction. It results from aberrant activation of macrophages and cytotoxic T cells, causing a cytokine storm.

Key Facts

  • Incidence: 1-2 cases per million adults annually (likely underdiagnosed)
  • Mortality: 40-50% in critically ill adults
  • Classification:
    • Primary (Familial) HLH: Genetic defects in cytotoxic function (rare in adults)
    • Secondary HLH: Triggered by infections, malignancy, or autoimmune disease (majority of adult cases)
ICU Data: In a systematic review of 148 critically ill adults with HLH, infections were the most common trigger (88.1%), followed by autoimmune disorders (19.7%) and haematological malignancies (13.6%). Mechanical ventilation was required in 83.3% of cases. (Montrucchio et al., 2025)

Pathophysiology

Mechanism of Disease

HLH results from failure to downregulate the immune response following an initial trigger. Defective cytotoxic T-cell and NK cell function leads to:

  1. Impaired target cell killing and failure to eliminate activated immune cells
  2. Persistent immune activation with continued stimulation of T cells and macrophages
  3. Cytokine storm with massive release of IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-18
  4. Uncontrolled macrophage proliferation and activation with hemophagocytosis
  5. Multi-organ dysfunction from direct tissue damage and consumptive coagulopathy

Aetiological Triggers

Infection-Associated (88%)

  • Viral: EBV (most common), CMV, HIV, SARS-CoV-2, HSV, Influenza
  • Bacterial: Tuberculosis, typhoid fever, Gram-negative sepsis
  • Parasitic: Leishmaniasis, malaria

Malignancy-Associated (14%)

  • T-cell and NK-cell lymphomas
  • B-cell lymphomas (including diffuse large B-cell lymphoma)
  • Acute leukaemias
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes

Autoimmune/Rheumatological (20%)

  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  • Adult-onset Still's disease (AOSD)
  • Systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis

Note: Often termed Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS) in rheumatological context

Iatrogenic

  • Post-transplant (solid organ or HSCT)
  • Immunosuppressive therapy
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors
  • CAR T-cell therapy
Clinical Pearl: In approximately 40% of cases, no clear trigger is identified despite thorough investigation. This should not delay treatment initiation when HLH is strongly suspected.

Clinical Presentation

Diagnostic Challenge: HLH presents with non-specific features that overlap significantly with sepsis. Maintain high index of suspicion in patients with unexplained multi-organ dysfunction, particularly with persistent fever and cytopenias.

Classic Presenting Features

Constitutional Symptoms (>95%)

  • Fever: Persistent or recurrent, typically >38.5°C
  • Night sweats
  • Weight loss
  • Profound fatigue

Haematological Manifestations (>90%)

Finding Typical Values Clinical Significance
Anaemia Hb <9 g/dL Progressive, often transfusion-dependent
Thrombocytopenia Platelets <100 × 10⁹/L Associated with bleeding risk; rapidly progressive
Neutropenia ANC <1.0 × 10⁹/L Increases infection susceptibility
Coagulopathy ↓ Fibrinogen, ↑ D-dimer Features of consumptive coagulopathy/DIC

Hepatic Dysfunction (>70%)

  • Hepatomegaly: Often with splenomegaly (hepatosplenomegaly)
  • Elevated transaminases: ALT/AST typically 2-5× upper limit normal
  • Hypertriglyceridaemia: Triglycerides often >3 mmol/L (>265 mg/dL)
  • Hepatic synthetic dysfunction: Low albumin, prolonged PT/INR

Neurological Manifestations (30-50%)

CNS involvement can be the presenting feature and is associated with poor prognosis:

  • Seizures (most common neurological feature - 48%)
  • Altered consciousness/confusion (36%)
  • Headache (21%)
  • Ataxia, focal deficits, encephalopathy
High-Risk Feature: CNS involvement significantly increases mortality. EBV may be limited to brain tissue in some cases, emphasising importance of CSF analysis. (Rajabi et al., 2025)

Red Flags for HLH

  • Persistent fever + bicytopenia/pancytopenia
  • Unexplained hepatosplenomegaly with transaminitis
  • Dramatically elevated ferritin (>1000 μg/L)
  • Marked hypertriglyceridaemia with fever
  • Multi-organ dysfunction without clear alternative diagnosis
  • Fever unresponsive to antibiotics in immunosuppressed patient

Diagnosis

Diagnosis requires high clinical suspicion combined with laboratory and histopathological findings. Two main diagnostic approaches are used:

HLH-2004 Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnostic Requirement: HLH diagnosis established by meeting 5 out of 8 clinical/laboratory features.

5 out of 8 of the following criteria must be met:

Criterion Threshold Notes
1. Fever ≥38.5°C Present in >95%; often persistent or recurrent
2. Splenomegaly Clinical or radiological Occurs in >70%; often with hepatomegaly
3. Cytopenias (≥2 lineages) Hb <9 g/dL
Platelets <100 × 10⁹/L
Neutrophils <1.0 × 10⁹/L		 Present in >95%; not secondary to marrow hypoplasia
4. Hypertriglyceridaemia and/or Hypofibrinogenaemia TG ≥3.0 mmol/L (≥265 mg/dL) OR
Fibrinogen ≤1.5 g/L		 TG in 60-75%; fibrinogen more specific
5. Hemophagocytosis In bone marrow, spleen, lymph nodes, or liver Neither sensitive NOR fully specific; absence does NOT rule out HLH
6. Low or absent NK cell activity Per local laboratory reference Highly specific (100%); may not be readily available
7. Ferritin ≥500 μg/L Often MUCH higher (>10,000 μg/L)
8. Soluble CD25 (sIL-2R) ≥2,400 U/mL Elevated in >95%; >10,000 U/mL highly suggestive (Sp 93%)
Important Limitations:
  • Hemophagocytosis is NOT required for diagnosis - it may be absent early or in extramedullary sites only
  • Some criteria (NK cell activity, sCD25) may not be routinely available
  • Do NOT delay treatment while awaiting all criteria

HScore

The HScore is a validated diagnostic calculator for adult patients with suspected secondary HLH.

HScore Advantages:
  • Specifically validated for adult populations
  • Provides probability rather than binary diagnosis

HScore Interpretation

  • <100: HLH very unlikely (<1%)
  • 100-150: Low suspicion (1-15%)
  • 150-169: Intermediate probability (15-40%) - consider empiric treatment
  • 169-250: High probability (40-90%) - strongly consider treatment
  • >250: Very high probability (>90%) - initiate HLH-directed therapy
Clinical Application: HScore ≥169 has 93% sensitivity and 86% specificity for HLH diagnosis. Given the high mortality of untreated HLH, empiric treatment should be strongly considered at this threshold.

Recommended Investigations

Essential Initial Tests

  • FBC with differential
  • Ferritin (markedly elevated, often >10,000 μg/L)
  • Triglycerides and fibrinogen
  • LFTs and coagulation screen
  • LDH
  • Blood film

Specialized Tests

  • Soluble IL-2 receptor (sCD25) - send even if not readily available
  • NK cell function (if available)
  • Bone marrow biopsy with flow cytometry

Infectious Workup

  • Blood cultures (bacterial and fungal)
  • Viral PCR: EBV, CMV, HSV, VZV, HHV-6, HIV, SARS-CoV-2
  • EBV serology and quantitative viral load
  • TB screening

Imaging

  • CT chest/abdomen/pelvis: assess organomegaly, lymphadenopathy
  • MRI brain if neurological symptoms
  • PET-CT: suspected malignancy

Lumbar Puncture

Consider if neurological signs/symptoms:

  • Cell count, protein, glucose
  • Microbiology culture and PCR (especially EBV)
  • Cytology

Initial Ward Management

⚠ Key Principle: HLH is a medical emergency. Early treatment initiation is associated with improved survival. Do NOT delay therapy while awaiting confirmatory tests if HLH is strongly suspected.

Immediate Actions

1. Multidisciplinary Team Involvement

Urgent involvement required:

  • Haematology: Mandatory - coordinate diagnostic workup and treatment
  • Infectious diseases: Identify and treat underlying infections
  • Rheumatology: If autoimmune trigger suspected
  • Intensive care: Early involvement if organ support likely
  • Neurology: If CNS involvement

2. Complete Diagnostic Workup

Initiate comprehensive investigations whilst starting treatment.

3. Identify and Treat Underlying Trigger

  • Infection: Start appropriate antimicrobials immediately
  • Malignancy: Urgent haematology/oncology involvement for chemotherapy
  • Autoimmune: Treat underlying condition

HLH-Directed Pharmacotherapy

Standard HLH-94/2004 Protocol

1. Dexamethasone

Week 1-2 10 mg/m² daily (divided doses)
Week 3-4 5 mg/m² daily
Week 5-6 2.5 mg/m² daily
Week 7-8 1.25 mg/m² daily

2. Etoposide

Weeks 1-2 150 mg/m² IV twice weekly
Weeks 3-8 150 mg/m² IV once weekly

3. Cyclosporine A (if CNS involvement or refractory)

Dose 3-5 mg/kg/day PO divided BID
Target trough 200-400 ng/mL

Modified Approaches for Adult Secondary HLH

High-Dose Corticosteroids Alone

May be appropriate for mild to moderate HLH:

Dexamethasone 10-20 mg daily IV/PO
OR Methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg/day IV (or pulse 500-1000 mg × 3-5 days)

Reassess at 3-5 days: If no improvement, escalate to etoposide

Alternative Agents

Anakinra (IL-1 antagonist)

Dose 100 mg SC daily (up to 4-8 mg/kg/day)
Use Particularly for MAS/autoimmune-triggered HLH

Ruxolitinib (JAK1/2 inhibitor)

Dose 10-20 mg PO BID
Use Steroid-refractory HLH; emerging data

Supportive Care

Infection Prophylaxis

PCP prophylaxis Co-trimoxazole 960 mg PO TDS weekly
Fungal prophylaxis Fluconazole 400 mg daily
Viral prophylaxis Aciclovir 400 mg PO BD

Blood Product Support

  • Red cells: Transfuse for Hb <70 g/L (or higher if symptomatic)
  • Platelets: Transfuse for Plt <10×10⁹/L or <50×10⁹/L if bleeding
  • Use irradiated products if potential HSCT candidate

Treatment Response Assessment

Positive response indicators:

  • Defervescence (fever resolves)
  • Declining ferritin (>20-30% reduction)
  • Improving cytopenias
  • Normalisation of triglycerides and fibrinogen

Criteria for ICU Transfer

Consider ICU transfer if:

  • Respiratory failure or increasing oxygen requirements
  • Haemodynamic instability or shock
  • Altered consciousness or deteriorating neurology
  • Acute kidney injury requiring RRT
  • Multi-organ dysfunction
  • Severe lactic acidosis (lactate ≥10 mmol/L)

ICU Management

Prognosis: Mortality rates approach 50% in critically ill adults with HLH. Factors associated with poor prognosis include viral infections (especially SARS-CoV-2), underlying haematological malignancy, and need for mechanical ventilation. Despite this, aggressive supportive care combined with HLH-directed therapy can lead to recovery.

Organ Support

Respiratory Support

Over 80% of ICU patients with HLH require mechanical ventilation.

Lung-Protective Ventilation (ARDS is common):

  • Tidal volume: 6 mL/kg ideal body weight
  • Plateau pressure: Target <30 cmH₂O
  • PEEP: Optimised using PEEP/FiO₂ table
  • Permissive hypercapnia: Accept pH >7.20

Prone Positioning: For severe ARDS (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150 mmHg), prone for ≥16 hours daily

ECMO: Consider for refractory hypoxaemia if reversible cause and HLH treatment initiated

Haemodynamic Support

Distributive shock is common in HLH:

  • Fluid resuscitation: Balanced crystalloids, avoid fluid overload
  • Vasopressors: Noradrenaline first-line
  • Vasopressin: Consider as second agent if refractory
  • Inotropes: Dobutamine if evidence of cardiac dysfunction
Haemodynamic Monitoring: Arterial line essential. Consider advanced monitoring if refractory shock.

Renal Support

AKI is common and frequently requires RRT:

  • CRRT preferred over intermittent HD in haemodynamically unstable patients
  • Indications: Standard criteria (refractory hyperkalaemia, acidosis, uraemia, fluid overload)
  • Anticoagulation: Regional citrate if Plt >30×10⁹/L and no severe liver dysfunction

Haematological Management

Severe Coagulopathy:

  • Monitor coagulation closely (PT, APTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer)
  • FFP/cryoprecipitate for active bleeding with consumptive coagulopathy
  • Platelet transfusions liberally if <50×10⁹/L and bleeding
  • Consider tranexamic acid for refractory bleeding (caution with DIC)

Severe Lactic Acidosis (≥10 mmol/L):

Extreme lactic acidosis is a rare but critical manifestation of HLH:

  • Etoposide can rapidly normalise lactate (within 24 hours in malignancy-associated HLH)
  • Aggressive HLH treatment is key
  • Standard supportive measures for acidosis
Clinical Pearl: Extreme lactic acidosis (lactate ≥10 mmol/L) in ICU patients should prompt urgent HLH evaluation. (Xu et al., 2025)

Infection Management in ICU

  • High index of suspicion: Patients are profoundly immunosuppressed
  • Early broad-spectrum antibiotics: For any clinical deterioration
  • Fungal coverage: Low threshold for antifungal therapy (invasive aspergillosis risk)
  • CMV reactivation: Monitor and treat pre-emptively
  • Daily clinical surveillance: New infiltrates, worsening organ function

Specific ICU Considerations

Sedation

  • Use standard ICU sedation protocols
  • Monitor for hepatic dysfunction affecting drug metabolism
  • Daily sedation holds when appropriate

Nutrition

  • Early enteral nutrition when tolerated
  • Avoid lipid-containing TPN if marked hypertriglyceridaemia
  • Patients are catabolic - adequate protein provision

VTE Prophylaxis

  • Risk vs benefit given coagulopathy and thrombocytopenia
  • Mechanical prophylaxis (TED stockings, intermittent pneumatic compression)
  • Pharmacological if Plt >50×10⁹/L and no active bleeding

Monitoring in ICU

Parameter Frequency
FBC Daily
Ferritin Twice weekly (key marker of response)
LFTs, coagulation Daily initially, then twice weekly
Triglycerides, fibrinogen Weekly
Lactate, ABG As clinically indicated
Cyclosporine level Twice weekly until stable

Prognostic Factors

Associated with increased mortality:

  • Viral infections (especially SARS-CoV-2)
  • Underlying haematological malignancy
  • Need for mechanical ventilation
  • Multi-organ failure
  • Delayed treatment initiation

Associated with improved outcomes:

  • Early diagnosis and treatment
  • Identification and control of underlying trigger
  • Corticosteroid use (in some studies)
  • Infection-associated HLH (better prognosis than malignancy-associated)
