Overview

Setting: Critical Care Units across all UHBW adult critical care units (GICU, CICU, WICU)

For Staff: Clinical staff working across Critical Care

Patients: All Critical Care patients

Objectives of Guideline and Algorithm

  1. Safely feed critically ill patients prior to dietetic assessment
  2. Early initiation of enteral nutrition (within 24 hours of admission)
  3. Initiate and build up energy, protein, and micronutrients with protocolised regimens
  4. Guide identification and management of refeeding syndrome
  5. Management of tolerance issues and appropriate feeding cessation
  6. Appropriate use of total and supplementary parenteral nutrition
  7. Guide oral nutrition support

Critical Care Context and Special Populations

Metabolic Phases in Critical Illness

Acute/Catabolic Phase (Days 1-7):

  • Hypermetabolic state with increased energy expenditure
  • Accelerated protein catabolism (muscle breakdown)
  • Insulin resistance and hyperglycaemia
  • Inflammatory response with cytokine release
  • Increased micronutrient requirements (vitamins C, E, selenium)
  • Nutritional approach: Permissive underfeeding (70-80% of target), prioritise early EN, avoid overfeeding

Recovery/Anabolic Phase (Day 7 onwards):

  • Resolving inflammation and metabolic stabilisation
  • Protein synthesis predominates
  • Improving insulin sensitivity
  • Restoration of lean body mass
  • Nutritional approach: Progress to full energy/protein targets, optimise anabolism

Special Clinical Contexts

Sedated and Ventilated Patients

  • Energy expenditure: Often reduced due to sedation (15-25% decrease)
  • Propofol calories: Must be counted - propofol provides 1.1 kcal/ml (e.g., 100ml/hr = 2,640 kcal/day)
  • Gastric emptying: Often delayed - monitor GRVs closely, consider prokinetics early
  • Position: Head of bed elevation 30-45° reduces aspiration risk
  • Extubation planning: Ensure adequate nutrition pre-extubation to support respiratory muscles

Patients on Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

  • Protein requirements: Increased to 1.5-2.5 g/kg/day (higher losses via dialysate/filtrate)
  • Amino acid losses: Approximately 10-15g lost per day of CRRT
  • Micronutrient losses: Water-soluble vitamins (especially thiamine, folate), trace elements
  • Fluid restriction: May require concentrated feeds (e.g., Nutrison Concentrated 2.0 kcal/ml)
  • Electrolytes: Monitor closely - potassium, phosphate, magnesium frequently deranged
  • Timing: Citrate-based CRRT may affect calcium/magnesium - adjust monitoring accordingly

Hepatic Dysfunction

  • Protein metabolism: Altered - may require modified amino acid profile
  • Encephalopathy risk: Standard protein intake usually tolerated; reduce only if encephalopathy worsens
  • Branched-chain amino acids (BCAA): Consider specialist feeds if severe encephalopathy
  • Micronutrients: Deficiencies common (thiamine, folate, zinc, vitamins A, D, E, K)
  • Glucose control: Risk of hypoglycaemia due to impaired gluconeogenesis
  • Fluid management: May require concentrated feeds if ascites/oedema present

Febrile Patients

  • Metabolic rate: Increases approximately 10% per 1°C rise above 37°C
  • Protein requirements: Increased catabolism - aim for upper target range
  • Fluid requirements: Increased insensible losses
  • Micronutrients: Increased vitamin C and zinc requirements
  • Energy calculation: Adjust targets upward if persistent fever (>38.5°C for >48 hours)

Post-Operative Patients

Elective Surgery:

  • Early EN within 24 hours if haemodynamically stable
  • Enhanced recovery protocols - early oral intake encouraged
  • Jejunostomy tubes (if placed) - follow specific protocol (see Jejunostomy tab)

Emergency Surgery (GI Pathology):

  • Higher risk of feed intolerance and anastomotic complications
  • Often require post-pyloric feeding
  • Longer period before establishing full feeds (5-7 days typical)
  • Consider supplementary PN if EN <60% by day 7

Malabsorption and GI Dysfunction

  • Short bowel syndrome: May require peptide feeds, PN supplementation
  • Pancreatic insufficiency: Use peptide feed (Nutrison Peptisorb Plus HEHP) + pancreatic enzyme replacement
  • High-output stoma/fistula: Fluid/electrolyte replacement, consider PN
  • Severe diarrhoea: Consider peptide feeds, reduce fibre content, exclude C. difficile
  • Gastroparesis: Post-pyloric feeding, prokinetics

Drug-Nutrient Interactions

Drug Interaction with Enteral Nutrition Management
Phenytoin Significantly reduced absorption when given with EN (up to 70% reduction) Stop feed 1-2 hours before and after dose. Monitor levels closely. Consider IV route or alternative anticonvulsant.
Warfarin Vitamin K content in feeds reduces warfarin effect Monitor INR closely. May require increased warfarin dose. Maintain consistent feeding pattern.
Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin) Chelation with divalent cations (calcium, magnesium) reduces absorption Stop feed 1 hour before and 2 hours after oral dose. IV route preferred in ICU.
Propofol Lipid emulsion provides 1.1 kcal/ml Calculate and subtract propofol calories from total energy prescription.
Suxamethonium/atracurium Gastric distension increases aspiration risk Stop feed before intubation/theatre. Aspirate gastric contents.
Lactulose May worsen diarrhoea when combined with fibre-containing feeds Consider low-fibre feed if diarrhoea problematic.

Referral to Dietitian

Mandatory Referrals via CareFlow EPR

The following patients must be referred to the dietitian via the CareFlow EPR system so that patients can be prioritised for nutritional assessment:

  1. Receiving enteral tube feeding - refer all tube feeds on day 1 of feeding
  2. Jejunostomy tube feeding post-oesophagogastric surgery - follow protocol for days 1-5
  3. Parenteral nutrition (supplementary and total) - by telephone before 1100hrs BRI / 1200hrs WICU
  4. Health professional concerns regarding nutritional status
  5. Patient or family concerns regarding nutritional status
  6. Chronic liver disease
  7. Acute pancreatitis
  8. Burns
  9. Tracheostomy
  10. Obesity (BMI >35 kg/m²)
  11. Complex care discharge planning

Standard Feeding Regimens

Continuous Overnight Regimen (Most Common)

Feed: Nutrison Protein Plus Multifibre 1.28 kcal/ml

  • Volume: 1000ml
  • Rate: 100ml/hr
  • Duration: 10 hours (usually 22:00-08:00)
  • Provides: 1,280 kcal and 62.5g protein per day

Advantages:

  • Allows mobilisation during day
  • Facilitates physiotherapy and procedures
  • Reduces aspiration risk (patient supine at night)
  • Standard approach in most ICUs

To increase intake:

  • Increase rate to 125ml/hr = 1,600 kcal, 78g protein
  • Or extend duration to 24 hours continuous
  • Or switch to concentrated feed

Bolus/Intermittent Regimen

Feed: Ensure Plus 1.5 kcal/ml (or equivalent)

  • Volume: 200-300ml per bolus
  • Frequency: 4-6 times daily (QDS to 6-hourly)
  • Typical schedule: 08:00, 12:00, 17:00, 22:00
  • Provides (200ml QDS): 1,200 kcal and 50g protein per day

Indications:

  • Step-down from continuous feeding
  • Transition to oral nutrition
  • Patients mobilising frequently
  • More physiological (mimics meals)

Contraindications:

  • Gastroparesis or delayed gastric emptying
  • High GRV risk
  • Post-pyloric feeding (must be continuous)
  • Critically unwell/sedated patients

Concentrated Feed Regimen (Fluid Restriction)

Feed: Nutrison Concentrated 2.0 kcal/ml

  • Rate: 40-60ml/hr
  • Provides (50ml/hr × 24hrs): 2,400 kcal and 90g protein
  • Volume: Only 1,200ml per day (vs 1,875ml for same calories with standard feed)

Indications:

  • Fluid restriction (cardiac/renal failure, ARDS)
  • Prone positioning (lower GRV threshold)
  • RRT with tight fluid balance

Important notes:

  • Lower protein concentration (15% vs 20% in Protein Plus)
  • May require protein supplement (e.g. Protifar) to meet targets
  • No fibre - higher risk of diarrhoea or constipation
  • More viscous - ensure adequate tube flushing

Feed Administration Guidelines

Tube flushing:

  • Flush with 30-50ml water before and after each feed
  • Flush every 4-6 hours during continuous feeding
  • Flush after medications (give separately from feeds if possible)
  • Use sterile water for jejunal tubes

Hang time:

  • Maximum 24 hours for closed systems (ready-to-hang bottles)
  • Maximum 4 hours for decanted feeds (opened in clinical area)
  • Label with patient details, date, and time prepared

Temperature:

  • Feeds should be given at room temperature
  • Do not refrigerate feeds once opened
  • Discard unused feed after 24 hours

Equipment:

  • Use dedicated feeding pump for continuous feeds
  • Change giving sets every 24 hours
  • Keep pump elevated above bed height to reduce reflux

Energy and Protein Requirements

Calculating Energy Requirements

Target Energy Intake: 20-25 kcal/kg/day (using actual body weight)

  • Acute phase (days 1-7): Permissive underfeeding at 70-80% of calculated target (typically 15-18 kcal/kg/day)
  • Recovery phase (day 7+): Progress to 100% of target energy (20-25 kcal/kg/day)
  • Obesity (BMI >30): Use adjusted body weight: IBW + 0.4(actual weight - IBW)
  • Indirect calorimetry: Gold standard when available - measures actual metabolic rate

Adjustments for Clinical Context:

  • Fever: Add 10% per 1°C rise above 37°C
  • Burns: 20-40 kcal/kg depending on total body surface area
  • Sedation: Reduce by 15-25%
  • Propofol: Subtract 1.1 kcal/ml from daily target

Protein Requirements

Standard ICU Target: 1.3 g/kg/day minimum

  • General critical illness: 1.3-1.5 g/kg/day
  • As clinically tolerated: Progress to 1.5-2.0 g/kg/day
  • Renal replacement therapy: 1.5-2.5 g/kg/day (compensates for losses)
  • Burns: 1.5-2.5 g/kg/day
  • Severe sepsis: 1.5-2.0 g/kg/day
  • Obesity (BMI >30): Use adjusted body weight for calculation

Contraindications to high protein:

  • Severe hepatic encephalopathy (reduce temporarily until stabilised)
  • Inborn errors of metabolism
  • Acute kidney injury without RRT (limit to 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day)

Evidence Base for ICU Nutrition

Timing of Enteral Nutrition

Recommendation: Initiate EN within 24-48 hours of ICU admission if haemodynamically stable.

Key Evidence:

  • Early EN (Doig et al., JAMA 2013): Systematic review showed early EN associated with reduced mortality and infectious complications
  • NUTRIREA-2 (Reignier et al., NEJM 2018): Early EN via gastric route was non-inferior to early EN via post-pyloric route
  • Haemodynamic stability: Avoid EN if on high/increasing vasopressor doses or evidence of gut ischaemia

Caloric Targets and Permissive Underfeeding

Recommendation: Target 70-80% of calculated energy requirements in first 7-10 days.

Key Evidence:

  • PermiT trial (Arabi et al., NEJM 2015): Permissive underfeeding (40-60% target) vs full feeding showed no difference in mortality but some benefits in duration of ventilation
  • EPaNIC trial (Casaer et al., NEJM 2011): Late PN (day 8+) superior to early PN (day 3) - suggests avoiding aggressive early nutrition
  • NUTRIREA-2 (2018): Full energy target not necessary in acute phase
  • Overfeeding risks: Hyperglycaemia, hepatic steatosis, increased CO₂ production (difficulty weaning), immune dysfunction

Route of Nutrition

Recommendation: Gastric feeding appropriate for most patients. Consider post-pyloric if high GRV or aspiration risk.

Key Evidence:

  • NUTRIREA-2 (2018): Gastric feeding non-inferior to post-pyloric feeding for clinical outcomes
  • Gastric advantages: Easier to place, maintains gastric function, physiological
  • Post-pyloric indications: Gastroparesis, high aspiration risk, pancreatitis, gastric surgery

Supplementary Parenteral Nutrition

Recommendation: Avoid early PN (<7 days). Consider supplementary PN after day 7-10 if EN achieving <60% of target.

Key Evidence:

  • EPaNIC trial (Casaer et al., NEJM 2011): Late PN (day 8+) associated with fewer infections, shorter ICU stay, lower costs compared to early PN
  • CALORIES trial (Harvey et al., JAMA 2014): Early PN vs early EN showed no mortality difference but PN group had more infections and longer ICU stay
  • Current practice: Allow time for EN to establish before adding PN

Gastric Residual Volumes

Recommendation: GRV threshold of 400ml (250ml if prone). Do not stop feeds for GRV <400ml.

Key Evidence:

  • ESPEN ICU Guidelines (2019): GRV >500ml should trigger intervention; <500ml generally safe
  • NUTRIREA-2 (2018): No GRV monitoring vs routine monitoring showed no difference in aspiration or pneumonia
  • Local practice: We monitor GRV but use higher threshold (400ml) to avoid unnecessary feed interruptions

Clinical Indicators

Signs of adequate nutrition:

  • Stable or improving biochemical markers
  • Wound healing progressing
  • Muscle strength improving (grip strength, respiratory muscle function)
  • Reduced vasopressor requirement
  • Successful weaning from ventilation

Signs of inadequate nutrition or overfeeding:

  • Persistent hyperglycaemia (>10 mmol/L) despite insulin
  • Rising triglycerides (>4.0 mmol/L)
  • Difficulty weaning from ventilator (high VCO₂)
  • Hepatic dysfunction (rising bilirubin/transaminases)
  • Persistent electrolyte derangement despite replacement

Dietetic Review

  • Mandatory referral criteria: See "Referral to Dietitian" section
  • Weekly review minimum: All patients receiving nutrition support for >7 days
  • Energy/protein assessment: Calculate percentage achieved vs target
  • Route optimisation: Consider transition from EN to oral, or from PN to EN
  • Discharge planning: Involve dietitians early for home feeding plans

Refeeding Syndrome

High-Risk Patients

Refeeding syndrome can be fatal. Identify high-risk patients:

  • BMI <16 kg/m²
  • Unintentional weight loss >15% in 3-6 months
  • Little or no nutritional intake for >10 days
  • Low baseline potassium, phosphate, or magnesium

Management Strategy

  1. Before feeding:
    • Check and correct electrolytes (K⁺, PO₄³⁻, Mg²⁺)
    • Prescribe thiamine 200-300mg daily for 10 days
    • Consider multivitamin and trace element supplementation
  2. During refeeding:
    • Slow calorie repletion (less than 50% protocol targets for first 72 hours)
    • Monitor fluid balance and vital signs during refeeding
    • Continue to monitor and correct electrolytes daily
  3. Refer to: UHBW Refeeding Syndrome Guideline for full protocol
National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE). Nutrition support for adults: oral nutrition support, enteral tube feeding and parenteral nutrition. Clinical guideline [CG32]. Published February 2006.

Gastric Residual Volume Management

GRV Monitoring Protocol

Standard Threshold: 400ml (250ml if patient in prone position)

Measurement Technique:

  • Aspirate gastric contents using 60ml syringe via NGT/gastrostomy
  • Measure volume in graduated container
  • Return aspirate to stomach (unless bilious, faeculent, or >500ml)
  • Document GRV on fluid balance chart

Frequency: Check 4-6 hourly for first 48 hours, then reduce if consistently low

Management Algorithm for Elevated GRV

GRV 250-400ml (or >250ml if prone):

  • Continue feeding at current rate
  • Return aspirate to stomach
  • Increase GRV monitoring frequency to 4 hourly
  • Optimise head of bed elevation (30-45°)
  • Review medications affecting gut motility

GRV 400-500ml (first occurrence):

  1. Continue feeding but increase monitoring to 2-4 hourly
  2. Start prokinetic therapy: Metoclopramide 10mg TDS IV/SC (first-line)
  3. Return aspirate to stomach unless faeculent/bilious
  4. Optimise patient positioning (semi-recumbent, right lateral)
  5. Review sedation - reduce if possible
  6. Exclude abdominal pathology (examine abdomen, check X-ray)

GRV >500ml or repeated GRV >400ml despite metoclopramide:

  1. Stop feed for 4 hours
  2. Add second prokinetic: Erythromycin 250mg QDS IV (second-line)
  3. Recheck GRV after 4 hours
  4. If GRV <250ml, restart feed at 50% of previous rate
  5. If GRV still >400ml, consider post-pyloric feeding (NJT/jejunostomy)
  6. Discuss with dietitian and medical team

Warning signs requiring urgent review:

  • Bilious or faeculent aspirate
  • Abdominal distension or pain
  • Absent bowel sounds
  • Vomiting (risk of aspiration)
  • Haemodynamic instability

Post-Pyloric Feeding Considerations

Indications for post-pyloric feeding:

  • Persistent high GRV (>400ml) despite dual prokinetic therapy
  • Recurrent vomiting or aspiration
  • Gastroparesis (documented delayed gastric emptying)
  • Severe acute pancreatitis
  • High aspiration risk (bulbar dysfunction, reduced consciousness)
  • Gastric surgery or anatomical abnormality

Post-pyloric feeding options:

  • Nasojejunal tube (NJT): Requires endoscopic or fluoroscopic placement
  • Surgical jejunostomy: Placed at time of surgery (see Jejunostomy Protocol tab)
  • Post-pyloric NG tube: Spontaneous migration beyond pylorus (unreliable)

Advantages of post-pyloric feeding:

  • Bypasses delayed gastric emptying
  • Lower aspiration risk
  • Can achieve higher feeding rates
  • No GRV monitoring required

Disadvantages:

  • Requires endoscopy/radiology for placement (NJT)
  • Higher risk of tube displacement
  • Cannot give medications via jejunal route
  • Requires continuous feeding (no bolus regimen)

Common Issues and Solutions

High Gastric Residual Volumes

  • Consider prokinetic agents (metoclopramide, erythromycin)
  • Elevate head of bed 30-45 degrees
  • Consider post-pyloric feeding if persistent

Diarrhoea

  • Review medications (particularly antibiotics)
  • Send stool sample for Clostridioides difficile testing
  • Ensure adequate fluid intake
  • Consider fibre-containing feed
  • Do not routinely stop enteral feeding for diarrhoea

Constipation

  • Ensure adequate fluid intake
  • Consider aperients as per bowel management protocol
  • Use fibre-containing feed where appropriate

When to Stop Feeding

Immediately discontinue feeding if:

  • Signs of bowel ischaemia
  • Severe abdominal distension or pain
  • Uncontrolled vomiting
  • Procedures requiring fasting (as per protocol)

Inform consultant and senior nursing staff immediately

Special Populations and Clinical Scenarios

Prone Positioning

Modifications for prone ventilation:

  • GRV threshold reduced to 250ml (vs 400ml in supine)
  • Feed rate: Consider using concentrated feed (Nutrison Concentrated 2.0 kcal/ml) to reduce volume
  • Monitoring: Check GRV every 4 hours minimum
  • Position: NG tube should be in optimal position - check X-ray if new tube or concerns
  • Aspiration risk: Higher due to positioning - maintain meticulous oral care
  • Before proning: Check GRV and aspirate stomach to minimise risk during turn

Typical regimen for prone patients:

  • Nutrison Concentrated 2.0 at 40-60ml/hr (1,920-2,880 kcal/24hr)
  • Provides high energy in lower volume (important for fluid restriction)
  • Protein content: 7.5g per 100ml

Severe Acute Pancreatitis

Nutritional management principles:

  • Early EN recommended: Start within 48-72 hours if haemodynamically stable
  • Route: Nasojejunal feeding preferred (post-pyloric) to minimise pancreatic stimulation
  • Feed type: Nutrison Peptisorb Plus HEHP 1.5 kcal/ml (changed from Peptisorb 1.0)
  • Peptide feeds: Easier to absorb, reduced pancreatic stimulation
  • Start low, go slow: Begin at 20ml/hr, increase by 10-20ml/hr every 12-24 hours as tolerated
  • Monitoring: Abdominal pain, amylase/lipase levels (may not correlate with tolerance), inflammatory markers

Important notes:

  • Gastric feeding can be used in mild pancreatitis if no ileus
  • PN only if EN not tolerated after 5-7 days or intestinal obstruction
  • Avoid high-fat feeds initially
  • Mandatory dietitian referral

Post-Whipple's Procedure (Pancreaticoduodenectomy)

Special considerations:

  • Route: Usually surgical jejunostomy placed at time of operation
  • Feed type: Nutrison Peptisorb Plus HEHP 1.5 kcal/ml (peptide feed)
  • Pancreatic Exocrine Insufficiency (PEI): Almost universal after Whipple's
  • Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT): Essential with feeds
  • Starting regimen: Follow jejunostomy protocol (see Jejunostomy Protocol tab)

PERT administration:

  • Creon capsules: Give with each feed bolus/start of continuous feed
  • Dose: Usually 25,000-50,000 units lipase per feed session
  • Cannot be given down jejunostomy - give via NGT or orally when taking
  • Monitor for steatorrhoea (fatty, pale, floating stools)

Transition to polymeric feeds:

  • Can transition from peptide to standard feed once tolerating full rate
  • Requires adequate PERT with polymeric feeds
  • Monitor fat-soluble vitamin levels (A, D, E, K)

Obesity (BMI >30)

Weight calculations:

  • Energy target: Use adjusted body weight (ABW)
  • Formula: ABW = IBW + 0.4 × (actual weight - IBW)
  • IBW calculation: Men: 50kg + 2.3kg per inch >5ft; Women: 45.5kg + 2.3kg per inch >5ft
  • Target: 20-25 kcal/kg ABW per day

Protein requirements:

  • Calculate using ABW
  • Target 1.5-2.0 g/kg ABW (higher protein needs to preserve lean body mass)

Special considerations:

  • Higher risk of aspiration (increased intra-abdominal pressure)
  • Often have comorbidities (diabetes, sleep apnoea, cardiovascular disease)
  • Micronutrient deficiencies common (vitamin D, thiamine, folate)
  • Avoid intentional weight loss during acute illness
  • Higher rates of delayed gastric emptying

Burns

Hypermetabolic response:

  • Energy requirements: 20-40 kcal/kg/day depending on % total body surface area (TBSA)
  • Small burns (<20% TBSA): 25-30 kcal/kg/day
  • Major burns (>20% TBSA): 30-40 kcal/kg/day
  • Protein requirements: 1.5-2.5 g/kg/day (severe catabolism)

Route and timing:

  • Early EN crucial (within 24 hours)
  • Gastric route usually tolerated
  • May require post-pyloric feeding if ileus or high GRV

Micronutrients:

  • Increased requirements: Vitamin C (wound healing), zinc (immune function), selenium, vitamin A
  • Consider supplementation beyond standard feed content

Monitoring:

  • Weekly nutritional assessment
  • Nitrogen balance if feasible
  • Weight (difficult due to oedema/fluid resuscitation)
  • Mandatory dietitian involvement

Tracheostomy Patients

Considerations for EN:

  • Swallowing assessment essential before oral intake
  • NG tube can remain in situ during oral trials
  • Cuff deflation timing coordinated with feeding (usually deflate between feeds)
  • Higher aspiration risk during transition to oral intake

Transition to oral nutrition:

  • Speech and Language Therapy (SALT) assessment required
  • Blue dye test or Fibreoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)
  • Gradual weaning from EN as oral intake increases
  • Overnight NG feeding often used during transition phase
  • See "Oral Nutrition Support" section for progression plan

Head Injury and Neurocritical Care

Special considerations:

  • Energy requirements: May be increased (hypermetabolism) but assess individually
  • Protein: 1.5-2.0 g/kg/day to counteract catabolism
  • Timing: Early EN (within 24-48 hours) associated with better outcomes
  • Gastric emptying: Often delayed - monitor GRVs closely, low threshold for prokinetics

Complications to monitor:

  • Hyperglycaemia (stress response, raised ICP affects hypothalamus)
  • Diabetes insipidus (fluid/sodium imbalance)
  • SIADH (hyponatraemia)
  • Autonomic dysfunction affecting gut motility

Micronutrients:

  • Consider thiamine supplementation (especially if alcohol-related injury)
  • Vitamin D often deficient
  • Selenium, zinc for antioxidant support

Parenteral Nutrition (PN)

Indications for PN

  • Non-functional or inaccessible gastrointestinal tract
  • Persistent feed intolerance despite optimisation
  • Intestinal failure
  • Severe malnutrition when enteral feeding inadequate
  • High output fistulae

PN Prescribing Requirements

All PN must be discussed with dietitian before prescribing:

  • BRI: Telephone before 1100hrs
  • WICU: Telephone before 1200hrs

Refer to: UHBW Prescribing and Provision of PN for Adult Inpatients guideline

Supplementary PN

Consider when enteral nutrition provides <50% of nutritional requirements after optimisation attempts, and patient expected to remain on critical care for >5 days.

Total PN

Required when enteral nutrition is contraindicated or not possible, and expected duration >5-7 days.

Oral Nutrition Support

Indications

  • Patient able to swallow safely (refer to Speech and Language Therapy if concerns)
  • Inadequate oral intake to meet nutritional requirements
  • Patient recovering from critical illness

Strategies

  1. Food-first approach:
    • High-energy, high-protein diet
    • Fortified foods and drinks
    • Small, frequent meals
    • Encourage patient choice and preference
  2. Oral nutritional supplements (ONS):
    • Prescribe when food alone is insufficient
    • Standard: 300kcal per serving (e.g., Ensure Plus, Fortisip)
    • Typically prescribe 2-3 servings per day
    • Best taken between meals, not as meal replacements

Monitoring Oral Intake

  • Food charts for patients at nutritional risk
  • Regular weight monitoring
  • Refer to dietitian if intake remains inadequate

Protocol for Feeding via Jejunostomy/Nasojejunal Tube Post-Oesophagogastric and Hepatobiliary Surgery

Protocol Scope

Setting: Intensive care units and surgical wards

For Staff: All clinical staff involved in caring for the patients below

Patients: All postoperative oesophagogastric (OG) and hepatobiliary (HPB) patients with a feeding jejunostomy or nasojejunal tube in situ

Aim

To safely commence and advance enteral nutrition via a jejunostomy/nasojejunal tube in postoperative oesophagogastric and hepatobiliary patients.

Important Information – Please Read Before Commencing Feeding

  • Complete relevant CareFlow EPR Service order: "Adult Nutrition and Dietetics Referral (BRISTOL PATIENTS) or (WESTON PATIENTS)" for all patients starting this protocol
  • If the patient is at risk of refeeding syndrome, refer to UHBW Refeeding Syndrome Guideline
  • Commence the appropriate feeding tube care plan:
    • Care Plan: Management of Nasojejunal Feeding Tubes
    • Care Plan: Post Insertion Management of Percutaneous Feeding Jejunostomy Tubes
  • Jejunostomy feeding tubes should not be used for the first 24 hours post insertion
  • If at any time your patient experiences abdominal bloating/cramping/distension or worsening/severe pain, immediately discontinue feeding until the patient has been reviewed by a consultant surgeon or registrar
  • If bowel ischaemia is suspected, appropriate investigations should be performed as directed by senior OG/HPB surgeon (e.g., blood gases, contrast CT scan)
  • The protocol covers days 1-5 of feeding. Feeding rate should only be increased faster than the protocol if documented by a consultant OG/HPB surgeon. If there is no evidence of ileus, consider increasing feed rate to a bespoke Dietitian regimen from Day 3 onwards
  • An alternative feed may be provided under the documented advice of a surgeon or dietitian

Special Considerations

Allergies

Nutrison Protein Plus Multifibre contains fish oils, milk protein and soya protein.

If a patient is allergic to fish, soy or milk DO NOT use this regimen and await dietetic input.

Cultural and Dietary Needs

This protocol feed is Halal and Kosher but not vegetarian or vegan.

Pancreatic Exocrine Insufficiency (PEI)

This feed is polymeric so at higher feed rates additional Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT) may be required for patients with Pancreatic Exocrine Insufficiency. Refer to UHBW clinical guideline "The Management Of Pancreatic Exocrine Insufficiency In Enteral Tube Feeding."

Diabetes

Diabetes medications/insulin may need reviewing with altered carbohydrate content and delivery of jejunal feed. Refer to the Diabetes Specialist Nurses by completing CareFlow EPR Service order "BRI Diabetes Specialist Nurse Inpatient Review."

Discharge Planning

Inform the Dietitian as soon as possible if the patient is going home with a feeding tube. This allows them to implement timely discharge planning and arrange training.

Drug-Nutrient Interactions

Be aware of possible drug-nutrient interactions. Consult with pharmacy.
